Gemstone & Craftsmanship Guide

A quiet guide to what makes fine jewellery last.

Fine jewellery is not only defined by the stone you see first. Its character comes from several quieter decisions: the metal, the setting, the cut, the proportions, and the way each element works with the person who will wear it.

This guide is designed to make those foundations easier to understand. Not as technical noise, but as a calm starting point for choosing pieces with more confidence.

Precious Metals and Longevity

The metal is the foundation of every piece. It shapes the colour, weight, durability and long-term feel of the jewellery. A beautiful design can catch the eye immediately, but the right metal determines how gracefully the piece will live over years of wear.

Gold is naturally soft in its purest form, which is why fine jewellery usually uses gold blended with other metals for strength. 18K gold contains a higher proportion of pure gold, giving it a richer tone and a more substantial luxury feel. It is often chosen for pieces where depth of colour and refinement matter most.

14K gold has a slightly lighter tone, but it offers excellent durability for everyday wear. For rings, bracelets and pieces that may be worn often, that balance of beauty and strength can be a practical advantage.

Platinum offers a different kind of permanence. Naturally white, dense and hypoallergenic, it does not need the same rhodium replating often used to maintain the bright finish of white gold. Over time, platinum develops a soft patina rather than simply looking worn. For many collectors, that quiet ageing is part of its appeal.

The Architecture of Settings

A setting is more than the way a stone is held in place. It changes the entire silhouette of a piece, the way it catches light, and how protected the gemstone feels in daily life.

A prong setting uses fine metal claws to lift the stone and allow light to enter from several angles. This is why it is often chosen for pieces where brilliance, fire and presence are important.

A bezel setting surrounds the edge of the stone with a smooth rim of metal. It offers a more protected, architectural look and is especially appealing for people who prefer clean lines or want a piece that feels secure for regular wear.

A pavé setting places small diamonds closely together across the surface of the metal, creating a continuous field of light. When done well, it does not feel excessive. It adds texture, softness and a refined shimmer that supports the main design rather than overwhelming it.

Understanding Gemstones

Diamonds and coloured gemstones are often discussed through similar words - colour, clarity, cut and carat - but they are not judged in exactly the same way.

With diamonds, the traditional 4Cs provide a useful structure. Colour, clarity, cut and carat weight help explain how a diamond is graded and why two stones that look similar at first can have very different values.

With coloured gemstones, the eye becomes more nuanced. A sapphire, emerald, ruby or aquamarine is not only valued by technical perfection. Its beauty often comes from the life of its colour: the purity of the hue, the balance of tone, and the way the stone responds in different light.

Colour is one of the most important signals. In diamonds, near-colourlessness is often prized. In coloured gemstones, the goal is different: richness, depth and a shade that feels alive rather than flat, grey or overly dark.

Clarity also works differently across stones. A very high level of clarity is especially important in diamonds because brilliance and transparency are central to their appeal. In many coloured gemstones, small natural inclusions are common and can be part of the stone’s character. They remind us that the material came from nature, not from a perfectly controlled surface.

Cut is where craftsmanship becomes visible. A thoughtful cut gives the stone proportion, movement and light. When the facets are arranged well, the gem does not only sparkle under showroom lighting. It keeps its presence in softer, everyday environments too.

Carat weight is often misunderstood. Carat measures weight, not visual size. Because gemstones have different densities, two stones with the same carat weight may appear different in scale. This is why proportion, setting and how the stone sits on the hand or neckline can matter as much as the number itself.

What Makes a Piece Feel Considered

The strongest jewellery rarely depends on one impressive feature alone. A large stone can feel heavy if the proportions are wrong. A rare gem can feel less refined if the setting competes with it. A simple design can feel exceptional when every detail is balanced.

A considered piece usually has a sense of restraint. The metal supports the stone. The setting protects without distracting. The scale feels appropriate. The design has enough presence to be noticed, but enough quietness to be worn naturally.

That balance is often what separates fine jewellery from jewellery that is simply expensive.
